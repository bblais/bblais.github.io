Fine Tuning

-- A Bayesian Approach (Part 2 of 2)

Thu 07 September 2023 In #statistics #Bayesian #religion

In a previous post I outline the fine tuning argument, and some of the reasons I find it unconvincing. In this post I walk through the same material but use probability theory to describe it, translating the math to pictures wherever I can. In some cases I think this approach makes the argument clearer, perhaps in other cases it make it more opaque -- your mileage may vary. At any rate, it should help a reader go through some of Luke Barnes's work like "A Reasonable Little Question: A Formulation of the Fine-Tuning Argument" which I read after doing the work for this post.

The setup

First, some notation: N≡not designed (i.e. naturalism)T≡designed (i.e. theism)ϕ≡a parameter in the theoryϕ∗≡the optimium value of a parameter in the theory \begin{aligned} N&\equiv \text{not designed (i.e. naturalism)} \\ T&\equiv \text{designed (i.e. theism)} \\ \phi&\equiv \text{a parameter in the theory} \\ \phi^\ast&\equiv \text{the optimium value of a parameter in the theory} \\ \end{aligned} NTϕϕ∗​≡not designed (i.e. naturalism)≡designed (i.e. theism)≡a parameter in the theory≡the optimium value of a parameter in the theory​ In the argument, we are trying to compare the two hypotheses, NNN and TTT, given the data. What is the data? There are two pieces: the existence of life and the value of the parameter of a theory, ϕ\phiϕ. This parameter represents the collection of parameters that are supposedly fine-tuned, listed in the previous post. Without loss of generality, we can collect the entire weight of the fine tuning to one fine-tuned uber-parameter, ϕ\phiϕ.

The comparison of the hypotheses can now be written as P(N∣life,ϕ)∼P(life,ϕ∣N)P(N)P(T∣life,ϕ)∼P(life,ϕ∣T)P(T) \begin{aligned} P(N|\text{life},\phi)&\sim P(\text{life},\phi|N)P(N) \\ P(T|\text{life},\phi)&\sim P(\text{life},\phi|T)P(T) \end{aligned} P(N∣life,ϕ)P(T∣life,ϕ)​∼P(life,ϕ∣N)P(N)∼P(life,ϕ∣T)P(T)​ where I've written only the numerator of Bayes Theorem using the same conventions used in my book "Statistical Inference for Everyone".

Marginalizing over the parameter ϕ\phiϕ we get

P(N∣life)∼∫ϕP(life∣ϕ,N)P(ϕ∣N)P(N)dϕP(T∣life)∼∫ϕP(life∣ϕ,T)P(ϕ∣T)P(T)dϕ \begin{aligned} P(N|\text{life})&\sim \int_\phi P(\text{life}|\phi,N)P(\phi|N)P(N) d\phi \\ P(T|\text{life})&\sim \int_\phi P(\text{life}|\phi,T)P(\phi|T)P(T) d\phi \end{aligned} P(N∣life)P(T∣life)​∼∫ϕ​P(life∣ϕ,N)P(ϕ∣N)P(N)dϕ∼∫ϕ​P(life∣ϕ,T)P(ϕ∣T)P(T)dϕ​ If we can take the priors to be equal, P(N)=P(T)=0.5P(N)=P(T)=0.5P(N)=P(T)=0.5 for the moment it is equivalent to ignoring the priors -- something apologists always do. The argument then becomes that the likelihood ratios are so skewed that no reasonable difference in priors could overcome it. This will come up again in the objections.

The argument

We have a number of terms in the equation above that factor into the argument.

  • priors are not relevant, and are taken to be equal, P(N)=P(T)=0.5P(N)=P(T)=0.5P(N)=P(T)=0.5
  • non-design (i.e. naturalism) has no preference for the parameter, ϕ\phiϕ over a wide or infinite range: P(ϕ∣N)P(\phi|N)P(ϕ∣N)
  • we derive from current theories that a very narrow range for the parameter ϕ∼ϕ∗\phi \sim \phi^\astϕ∼ϕ∗ is needed to produce life: both P(life∣ϕ,N)P(\text{life}|\phi,N)P(life∣ϕ,N) and P(life∣ϕ,T)P(\text{life}|\phi,T)P(life∣ϕ,T)
  • design (i.e. theism) predicts that the parameter can be set externally to the universe by a designer at creation, and that life is a goal of creation, so it is likely to be set to a value conducive to life: P(ϕ∣T)P(\phi|T)P(ϕ∣T)

We show how the terms in the equation above map to these statements of the argument, using rough pictures of the probability functions. I use the XKCD style for the plots.

A short digression on the integral

The marginalization step we introduced to "get rid of" the parameter, ϕ\phiϕ, is the way to take into account the uncertainty of the parameter in a rigorous way. Mathematically it is expressed as an integral, ∫ϕP(life∣ϕ,N)P(ϕ∣N)dϕ \begin{aligned} \int_\phi P(\text{life}|\phi,N)P(\phi|N) d\phi \end{aligned} ∫ϕ​P(life∣ϕ,N)P(ϕ∣N)dϕ​ As a picture it is easy to see it as the percent overlap of the two functions, as shown here:

The setup of the problem using probability

The fine-tuning argument is set up as a straightforward model selection problem. Let's define the models:

  • RRR - random
  • NNN - necessity
  • GGG - design (this is essentially God)

So the comparison is between P(R∣data)P(R|\rm data)P(R∣data), P(N∣data)P(N|\rm data)P(N∣data), and P(G∣data)P(G|\rm data)P(G∣data).

P(R∣life,tuning)∼P(life,tuning∣R)P(R)=P(life∣tuning,R)P(tuning∣R)P(R)P(R|\rm life, tuning) \sim P(\rm life,tuning|R)P(R) = P(\rm life|\rm tuning,R)P(\rm tuning|R)P(R)P(R∣life,tuning)∼P(life,tuning∣R)P(R)=P(life∣tuning,R)P(tuning∣R)P(R)

The three equations would then be

P(R∣life,tuning)∼P(life∣tuning,R)P(tuning∣R)P(R)P(N∣life,tuning)∼P(life∣tuning,N)P(tuning∣N)P(N)P(G∣life,tuning)∼P(life∣tuning,G)P(tuning∣G)P(G) \begin{aligned} P(R|\rm life, tuning) &\sim P(\rm life|\rm tuning,R)P(\rm tuning|R)P(R) \\ P(N|\rm life, tuning) &\sim P(\rm life|\rm tuning,N)P(\rm tuning|N)P(N) \\ P(G|\rm life, tuning) &\sim P(\rm life|\rm tuning,G)P(\rm tuning|G)P(G) \\ \end{aligned} P(R∣life,tuning)P(N∣life,tuning)P(G∣life,tuning)​∼P(life∣tuning,R)P(tuning∣R)P(R)∼P(life∣tuning,N)P(tuning∣N)P(N)∼P(life∣tuning,G)P(tuning∣G)P(G)​
