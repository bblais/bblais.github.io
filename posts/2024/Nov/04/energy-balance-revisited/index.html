Energy Balance Revisited

Mon 04 November 2024 In #science

Going back a few years I published an article simplifying the calculations for atmospheric greenhouse gas effects for undergraduate non-science majors,

Blais, B. (2003). Teaching energy balance using round numbers. Physics education, 38(6), 519.

I recently came across another article which does a similar thing, but using slightly different notation and terminology,

LoPresto, M. C. (2013). Adding albedo and atmospheres. The Physics Teacher, 51(3), 152-153.

In the spirit of reproducibility I wanted to see if I could connect LoPresto's results with mine.

Round Number Method

In my method, the amount of energy entering the Earth system is the round number S=100/r2S=100/r^2S=100/r2 where rrr is the distance from the sun in astronomical units (AU). In these units, r=1r=1r=1 for the Earth. The temperature of the surface, or any atmospheric layer, is related to the energy output of that layer, derived to be (T88.5K)4=E \left(\frac{T}{88.5 \text{K}}\right)^4 = E (88.5KT​)4=E We look at a simple 1-layer model, where the short-wavelength albedo is aaa and the atmosphere absorbs some fraction, γ\gammaγ, of the long-wavelength radiation.

we get energy balance equations of

+(1−a)⋅S−Es+E1=0+γEs−E1−E1=0 \begin{aligned} +(1-a)\cdot S -E_s + E_1 &=0 \\ +\gamma E_s - E_1 - E_1 &=0 \end{aligned} +(1−a)⋅S−Es​+E1​+γEs​−E1​−E1​​=0=0​

which leads to solutions for the energy emitted from the surface and the layer 1, as well as the corresponding temperatures,

E1=γS⋅(1−a)2−γEs=2E1γ=2S⋅(1−a)2−γT1=88.5 K⋅(γS⋅(1−a)2−γ)1/4Ts=88.5 K⋅(2S⋅(1−a)2−γ)1/4=T1⋅21/4⋅(2−γ)−1/4 \begin{aligned} E_1&=\frac{\gamma S \cdot (1-a)}{2-\gamma} \\ E_s&=\frac{2E_1}{\gamma}=\frac{2 S \cdot (1-a)}{2-\gamma}\\ T_1&=88.5\text{ K}\cdot \left(\frac{\gamma S \cdot (1-a)}{2-\gamma}\right)^{1/4} \\ T_s&=88.5\text{ K}\cdot \left(\frac{2 S \cdot (1-a)}{2-\gamma}\right)^{1/4} = T_1 \cdot 2^{1/4}\cdot (2-\gamma)^{-1/4} \end{aligned} E1​Es​T1​Ts​​=2−γγS⋅(1−a)​=γ2E1​​=2−γ2S⋅(1−a)​=88.5 K⋅(2−γγS⋅(1−a)​)1/4=88.5 K⋅(2−γ2S⋅(1−a)​)1/4=T1​⋅21/4⋅(2−γ)−1/4​

We can check a few extreme cases, like γ=0\gamma=0γ=0 (no atmosphere), E1=0Es=S⋅(1−a)Ts=88.5 K⋅(S⋅(1−a))1/4 \begin{aligned} E_1&=0 \\ E_s&=S \cdot (1-a) \\ T_s&=88.5\text{ K}\cdot \left(S \cdot (1-a)\right)^{1/4} \end{aligned} E1​Es​Ts​​=0=S⋅(1−a)=88.5 K⋅(S⋅(1−a))1/4​ and an infrared-opaque atmosphere, γ=1\gamma=1γ=1,

E1=S⋅(1−a)Es=2S⋅(1−a)T1=88.5 K⋅(S⋅(1−a))1/4Ts=88.5 K⋅(2S⋅(1−a))1/4 \begin{aligned} E_1&=S \cdot (1-a) \\ E_s&=2 S \cdot (1-a)\\ T_1&=88.5\text{ K}\cdot \left(S \cdot (1-a)\right)^{1/4} \\ T_s&=88.5\text{ K}\cdot \left(2S \cdot (1-a)\right)^{1/4} \\ \end{aligned} E1​Es​T1​Ts​​=S⋅(1−a)=2S⋅(1−a)=88.5 K⋅(S⋅(1−a))1/4=88.5 K⋅(2S⋅(1−a))1/4​ Using the numbers from LoPresto, M. There are several things to note here. If we interpret γ\gammaγ as the fraction absorbed, both γ<0\gamma<0γ<0 and γ>1\gamma>1γ>1 are physically impossible. The former indicates that there is no atmosphere and we must be within uncertainties in the albedo or observed temperature. The latter is something we can address, by modifying the model. This is done by deriving the equations used in LoPresto, M. C. (2013).

Adding Albedo and Atmosphere

From the paper,

LoPresto, M. C. (2013). Adding albedo and atmospheres. The Physics Teacher, 51(3), 152-153.

we are given the result from a multi-layer model atmosphere. Their result for the case without an atmosphere is Te=(1−a)1/4(279r1/2) K T_e=(1-a)^{1/4}\left(\frac{279}{r^{1/2}}\right)\text{ K} Te​=(1−a)1/4(r1/2279​) K which we call TeT_eTe​ for effective radiative temperature.^[LoPresto calls it simply TTT which we find unclear.]. This is the same as our γ=0\gamma=0γ=0 case, Ts=88.5 K⋅(S⋅(1−a))1/4Ts=88.5 K⋅(100r2⋅(1−a))1/4=279 K⋅1r1/2⋅(1−a)1/4 \begin{aligned} T_s&=88.5\text{ K}\cdot \left(S \cdot (1-a)\right)^{1/4}\\ T_s&=88.5\text{ K}\cdot \left(\frac{100}{r^2} \cdot (1-a)\right)^{1/4}\\ &=279\text{ K} \cdot \frac{1}{r^{1/2}}\cdot \left(1-a\right)^{1/4} \end{aligned} Ts​Ts​​=88.5 K⋅(S⋅(1−a))1/4=88.5 K⋅(r2100​⋅(1−a))1/4=279 K⋅r1/21​⋅(1−a)1/4​ They then include a greenhouse, and derive the temperature to be Tg=21/4⋅Te T_g = 2^{1/4}\cdot T_e Tg​=21/4⋅Te​ which is equivalent to our case above with γ=1\gamma=1γ=1. They then quote a result, Tg=(1+τ)1/4⋅Te T_g = (1+\tau)^{1/4}\cdot T_e Tg​=(1+τ)1/4⋅Te​ without derivation, of which their previous result is a special case (τ=1\tau=1τ=1). The value τ\tauτ is called the "total extinction thickness" and is the number of layers that completely absorb infrared radiation (i.e. long wavelength). So there must be some connection between my γ\gammaγ and their τ\tauτ.

Deriving the total extinction thickness, τ\tauτ

To derive their result, we add more layers to the original model, and assume that every layer absorbs 100% of the radiation entering it from other layers.

The energy balance equations have for nnn such layers is,

+(1−a)⋅S−Es+E1=0+Es−E1−E1+E2=0+E1−E2−E2+E3=0⋮+En−3−En−2−En−2+En−1=0+En−2−En−1−En−1+En=0+En−1−En−En=0 \begin{aligned} +(1-a)\cdot S -E_s + E_1 &=0 \\ +E_s - E_1 - E_1 + E_2&=0 \\ +E_1 - E_2 - E_2 + E_3&=0 \\ &\vdots\\ +E_{n-3} - E_{n-2} - E_{n-2} + E_{n-1}&=0 \\ +E_{n-2} - E_{n-1} - E_{n-1} + E_n&=0 \\ +E_{n-1} - E_{n} - E_{n}&=0 \end{aligned} +(1−a)⋅S−Es​+E1​+Es​−E1​−E1​+E2​+E1​−E2​−E2​+E3​+En−3​−En−2​−En−2​+En−1​+En−2​−En−1​−En−1​+En​+En−1​−En​−En​​=0=0=0⋮=0=0=0​ You can confirm that this works for the n=3n=3n=3 model shown. Notice that there is an asymmetry in the equations, with the surface-layer and the top-layer equations a little different from the others.

Solving from the top layer (last equation) down to the surface we get En−1=2EnEn−2=2En−1−En=3EnEn−3=2En−2−En−1=4En⋮E2=(1+n−2)⋅EnE1=(1+n−1)⋅EnEs=(1+n)⋅En \begin{aligned} E_{n-1}&=2E_n \\ E_{n-2}&=2E_{n-1}-E_n = 3E_n \\ E_{n-3}&=2E_{n-2}-E_{n-1} = 4E_n \\ &\vdots\\ E_{2}&=(1+n-2)\cdot E_n\\ E_{1}&=(1+n-1)\cdot E_n\\ E_{s}&=(1+n)\cdot E_n \end{aligned} En−1​En−2​En−3​E2​E1​Es​​=2En​=2En−1​−En​=3En​=2En−2​−En−1​=4En​⋮=(1+n−2)⋅En​=(1+n−1)⋅En​=(1+n)⋅En​​ From the first balance equation we +(1−a)⋅S−Es+E1=0(1−a)⋅S−(1+n)⋅En+(1+n−1)⋅En=0(1−a)⋅S−En=0En=(1−a)⋅S \begin{aligned} +(1-a)\cdot S -E_s + E_1 &=0 \\ (1-a)\cdot S - (1+n)\cdot E_n + (1+n-1)\cdot E_n&=0 \\ (1-a)\cdot S-E_n&=0 \\ E_n&=(1-a)\cdot S \end{aligned} +(1−a)⋅S−Es​+E1​(1−a)⋅S−(1+n)⋅En​+(1+n−1)⋅En​(1−a)⋅S−En​En​​=0=0=0=(1−a)⋅S​

Our final result for the energy output of the surface in the multi-layer model is,

Es=(1+n)⋅(1−a)⋅STs=88.5 K⋅(1+n)1/4⋅((1−a)⋅S)1/4=(1+n)1/4⋅Te=(1+τ)1/4⋅Te \begin{aligned} E_{s}&=(1+n)\cdot (1-a)\cdot S \\ T_s &=88.5 \text{ K}\cdot (1+n)^{1/4}\cdot \left((1-a)\cdot S\right)^{1/4} \\ &=(1+n)^{1/4} \cdot T_e \\ &=(1+\tau)^{1/4} \cdot T_e \\ \end{aligned} Es​Ts​​=(1+n)⋅(1−a)⋅S=88.5 K⋅(1+n)1/4⋅((1−a)⋅S)1/4=(1+n)1/4⋅Te​=(1+τ)1/4⋅Te​​ Where the last step is the result from LoPresto, where it is clear that the number of layers, nnn, is equivalent to their "total extinction thickness", τ\tauτ.

Connecting our γ\gammaγ to LoPresto's τ\tauτ

Connecting my γ\gammaγ to LoPresto's τ\tauτ we get

22−γ=1+τ \frac{2}{2-\gamma}=1+\tau 2−γ2​=1+τ or γ=2τ1+τ \gamma=\frac{2\tau}{1+\tau} γ=1+τ2τ​ We can then reproduce the table above

The interpretation of, say, Venus's τ=98\tau=98τ=98 (100 effective layers!) is much easier using this result rather than the physically unreasonable γ=1.98\gamma=1.98γ=1.98. In teaching this concept to students again, I may sketch the derivation, and then quote the result:

Es=(1+n)⋅(1−a)⋅S \begin{aligned} E_{s}&=(1+n)\cdot (1-a)\cdot S \end{aligned} Es​​=(1+n)⋅(1−a)⋅S​ which has a reasonably intuitive simple form. I imagine there may be other useful comparisons to make.
